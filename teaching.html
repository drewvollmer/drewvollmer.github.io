Teaching Statement


Objectives

What skills make an economist? We don't passively accept models and memorize results—we build models, scrutinize their assumptions, and evaluate them analytically and empirically. My objective is for students to learn to do the same.

Methods

What methods help students master the economist's toolkit? I relied on five when teaching intermediate microeconomics.

  1. Clear explanations in lecture

    Clarity is my first priority when planning lectures. Several course evaluations for intermediate micro mention clear lectures.

  2. In-class practice

    I often replace lectured examples with in-class exercises. The exercises help students learn by giving them a chance to practice new ideas and work through any confusion. Students work on their own for a few minutes before their solutions in small groups. I monitor the groups and answer questions throughout.

  3. Questions with twists

    For students to master economic reasoning, they must practice it. On problem sets and exams, questions should not only ask if students understand key models, but also ask them to critique the models and apply them in new ways. For instance, in question 5 of the final asks students to apply the principles of decision making under risk—studied in insurance markets—to a different, but economically equivalent setting. The goal is to push students away from memorization and towards economic reasoning.

  4. Emphasis on modeling principles

    As with problem sets, lectures should explicitly emphasize the skills most important for students. Rather than just present results for a series of models, the lecturer should show how the models share a common structure, how the same methods of analysis apply to each model, and how the resulting equations have an economic interpretation.

  5. User-friendly background material

    Intermediate micro relies heavily on calculus, but economics courses should be accessible for less prepared and less technical students. To help them, I wrote a primer covering all of the math needed for the course with step-by-step explanations.

Assessing Students
  • In-class practice and discussions let me talk with students to learn what is most difficult. If everyone is confused by the same thing, I can address it immediately.
  • Problem sets and exams show when students repeat similar mistakes, which I can address in lecture and discussion.
Improvement
  • Professional development: I am enrolled in Duke's Certificate in College Teaching, which includes coursework and observation.
  • Mid-term course evaluations: I used a mid-term survey to listen to student opinions and adjust my micro course. I plan to ask again during my next courses.
  • Revising course materials: I followed a traditional structure for my micro class, but plan to tweak it to emphasize types of analysis, like Nash equilibrium, that are critical in economics but not required for the major.

Experience

  • Instructor, ECON 205D (Intermediate Microeconomics with Calculus), 2018

    Ran lecture and discussion, assigned problem sets, wrote and graded exams, and held office hours. 13 students (mainly economics majors).

  • Teaching Assistant, ECON 701/705 (PhD Microeconomics), 2016 -- 2017

    Ran weekly discussion sections and office hours, graded problem sets and exams, edited lecture notes, wrote exam questions, and gave feedback on draft exams. Coordinated with instructor each week about course schedule, problem sets, and questions on material. Coordinated with another TA weekly about discussion section content and grading. Both courses had 25 economics PhD students. 701 had 25 and 705 had 10 master's students and PhD students from other departments.

  • Teaching Assistant, Regression Analysis (as an undergraduate), 2012

    Ran office hours and graded problem sets with one other TA. Met with faculty weekly to discuss problem sets and solutions. Course had about 60 students, mainly economics majors.

  • Peer Tutor/Head Math Tutor (as an undergraduate), 2010 -- 2013

    Ran office hours for calculus I/II and intermediate macro, tutored one-on-one for anything and everything (most frequently intermediate macro and multivariable calculus). Organized the calculus tutoring schedule as head math tutor from Fall 2012 to Spring 2013.
